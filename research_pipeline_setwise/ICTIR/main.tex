\title{Finding the Worst Is Harder Than Finding the Best: Directional Asymmetry in LLM Setwise Ranking}

\author{Anonymous}

\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as effective zero-shot document rerankers. Among the four prompting paradigms---pointwise, pairwise, listwise, and setwise---the setwise approach offers a compelling trade-off between effectiveness and efficiency by asking the LLM to select the most relevant document from a candidate set. However, current setwise methods only extract one piece of ranking information per LLM call: the \emph{best} document. In this paper, we investigate \emph{bidirectional} selection strategies that extract more information from each comparison. We propose three approaches: (1) \textbf{Bottom-Up Setwise Ranking}, which reverses the selection to identify the \emph{least} relevant document; (2) \textbf{Dual-End Setwise Ranking}, which asks the LLM to simultaneously identify both the most and least relevant documents; and (3) \textbf{Bidirectional Ensemble}, which fuses top-down and bottom-up rankings. We pair the dual-end strategy with two new sorting algorithms---cocktail shaker sort and double-ended selection sort---designed for dual-output comparators. Experiments on TREC DL 2019/2020 with nine LLMs spanning three architecture families (Flan-T5, Qwen3, and Qwen3.5, ranging from 780M to 27B parameters) reveal a clearer story than the original ``three equally promising directions'' framing would suggest. The headline result is one of \emph{directional asymmetry}: the \dualend{} family is the strongest overall, with \dualend{}-Cocktail achieving the best mean NDCG@10 across both datasets and DualEnd methods (Cocktail or Selection) winning 14 of 18 model--dataset configurations; \bottomup{} alone is consistently and sometimes catastrophically weaker than \topdown{}, with 6 Bonferroni-significant losses; and \bidir{} ensemble does not outperform \topdown{} (3 Bonferroni-significant losses, 0 wins). Per-query significance tests are conservative: only one DualEnd gain remains Bonferroni-significant on these 43/54-query test sets, so we frame DualEnd as a quality-first refinement, not a free improvement. The mechanism we point to is positional: standalone worst-selection is dominated by a strong recency bias (the model labels the last passage as ``worst'' 40--63\% of the time), while the same model placed in a joint best-and-worst prompt produces a \emph{reversed} primacy-heavy ``dual\_worst'' pattern that is consistent across all 9 models. Joint elicitation therefore changes the model's behavior, not just its output channel. Our information-theoretic analysis shows that dual-end extracts $\sim$$74$--$90$\% more ranking information per LLM call at negligible prompt overhead, and a quality--cost Pareto analysis identifies the empty region between \topdown{}-Bubble and \dualend{}-Cocktail as the natural target for selective and bias-aware DualEnd refinements.
\end{abstract}
