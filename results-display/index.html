Step 1 / 4 stable baseline

    Current Evidence

    What the data supports, what it rejects, and why it still matters

    Supported

    DualEnd is the strongest family overall

    DualEnd is positive in 14/18 TREC DL configurations and wins all 12/12 Qwen configurations, even though the corrected significance is still limited on these small query sets.

    Rejected

    Worst-only is not a mirror of best-only

    BottomUp loses in all 18 configurations and has 6 Bonferroni-significant losses. The worst prompt is not a simple symmetric inverse of the best prompt.

    Why it matters

    The interesting signal is joint elicitation

    The key novelty is behavioral: asking for best and worst together changes the model's bias pattern and can add a modest refinement signal that does not survive when worst-selection is isolated.

    DualEnd positive deltas 14 / 18
    BottomUp Bonferroni-significant losses 6 / 18
    BiDir Bonferroni-significant losses 3 / 18
    Behavioral Fingerprint

    The current mechanism story in one screen

    Agreement

    DualEnd stays close to TopDown

    Overlap@10 = 7.01 and Kendall tau = 0.9254 between TopDown and DualEnd. That is much closer than TopDown vs BottomUp.

    Bias

    dual_worst flips the positional pattern

    Standalone worst-selection leans hard toward the last passage, but dual_worst shifts toward primacy instead: A ≈ 0.35, D ≈ 0.14.

    Interpretation

    Worst helps only when it shares the same prompt

    Independent BottomUp hurts. Independent fusion hurts. Same-call best-plus-worst is the only version that looks meaningfully useful.

    Completed Analysis

    The full non-BEIR result analysis

    Everything completed is surfaced below: ablations, parse robustness, per-query behavior, query difficulty, ranking agreement, position bias, Pareto tradeoffs, and qualitative help/hurt analysis. BEIR is intentionally excluded for now.

    Ablations

    Window size and passage length are family-dependent

    T5 prefers smaller windows and plateaus by 128 tokens, while Qwen models tolerate larger windows and only plateau around 256--512 tokens.

    Fusion

    BiDir only looks good when it becomes almost TopDown

    Weighted fusion is best at alpha=0.9, which means 90% of the signal still comes from TopDown. CombSUM does not rescue the weaker BottomUp channel.

    Robustness

    Parsing is not driving the story

    T5 avoids parsing entirely via likelihood scoring, and the Qwen family stays below a 1% partial parse rate even in the worst case.

    Diagnostics

    Bias, agreement, and wins tell one coherent story

    DualEnd stays close to TopDown, BottomUp diverges more, and the divergence behaves like systematic noise rather than useful complementarity.

    Pareto

    The quality-cost frontier is sharp, not fuzzy

    On mean TREC DL tradeoffs, the frontier is basically TD-Heap, TD-Bubble, and DE-Cocktail, with PermVote(p=2) only carving out one low-time niche.

    Qualitative

    DualEnd usually helps, but not in the same way everywhere

    Efficiency Analysis

    DualEnd carries a real budget cost, not a free information gain

    The current evidence says the same thing in three different ways: DualEnd extracts more information per comparison, but the end-to-end ranking process still pays materially more in calls, tokens, and wall time. That tradeoff needs to be shown directly, not implied.

    Interpretation

    More information per call does not mean cheaper ranking

    • For the default 4-way window, a single-direction call yields log2(4)=2.0 bits, while DualEnd yields log2(12)=3.58 bits, about 79% more information per comparison.
    • DE-Cocktail is still a real budget jump: 7.14x comparisons, 7.18x tokens, and 7.48x wall time versus TD-Heap.
    • DE-Selection is the genuine middle point: cheaper than cocktail on all three axes, but also weaker than both DE-Cocktail and TD-Bubble in mean quality.
    • BU-Bubble is the operational outlier: it is the most expensive method by far and still underperforms the TopDown family.
    • The correct paper framing is therefore quality-first, not efficiency-first: the joint signal helps, but the current algorithms do not reduce total budget.
    Operational Notes

    Prompt volume dominates, and model family details matter

    • T5 DualEnd-Cocktail uses likelihood scoring internally: for example, flan-t5-xl DL19 logs about 224,421 prompt tokens, 0 completion tokens, and 29.9s per query.
    • Qwen DualEnd pays small completion-token overhead but large latency: qwen3-8b DL19 logs about 228,901 prompt tokens, 4,887 completion tokens, and 188.2s per query.
    • Large Qwen3.5 models amplify the time cost most sharply: qwen3.5-27b DL20 logs about 239,690 total tokens and 637.3s per query for DE-Cocktail.
    • That means completion tokens are not the main story. The dominant costs are repeated window comparisons, large prompt volume, and slow causal generation on the larger models.

    This also explains why completion-token accounting needs a note: zero completion tokens for T5 DualEnd cocktail is a design consequence of likelihood scoring, not a counting bug.

    Proposed Refinements

    Three concrete follow-up methods suggested by the current evidence

    The data does not suggest "more BottomUp." It suggests spending the joint best-worst signal more carefully, making it less order-sensitive, or using the worst output only as a local constraint instead of a full second ranking channel.

    P1 · Main refinement

    Selective TopDown-DualEnd

    Keep the cheaper TopDown ranking backbone, but upgrade only selected windows to a same-call best-worst prompt.

    What

    The algorithm still behaves like TopDown most of the time. A window is switched to DualEnd only when the current local decision looks important or ambiguous, so the expensive joint signal is not paid everywhere.

    Why

    Full DualEnd is the best family overall, but the quality gain is modest relative to its cost. The qualitative and model-level results suggest that the gain is concentrated near hard decisions, especially close to the ranking head.

    Intuition

    Use cheap best-only ranking when the answer is already obvious. Spend joint best-worst reasoning only where the model might otherwise make an expensive mistake near top-k.

    Gating Strategies

    The implementation already exposes three interpretable routing rules through --gate_strategy.

    Shortlist gate

    Trigger DualEnd only on windows that overlap the current top shortlist, for example the top 20 candidates. This spends compute where a wrong decision can still affect the final answer.

    Uncertainty gate

    Trigger DualEnd only when the BM25 score spread inside the active window is small, using a margin threshold such as 0.15. This targets windows where lexical evidence alone does not cleanly separate candidates.

    Hybrid gate

    Use the union of shortlist overlap and uncertainty. This is the most paper-friendly default because it matches two intuitive ideas at once: top-k sensitivity and local ambiguity.

    P1 · Robustness refinement

    Order-Robust, Bias-Aware DualEnd

    Run DualEnd on a small set of controlled window orderings and majority-vote the best/worst decisions back into the original order.

    What

    On gated hard windows, compare the same candidate set in the base order, reversed order, and a small shifted order. Then remap the answers and vote on the final best and worst labels.

    Why

    The measured dual_worst pattern suggests that the joint signal is useful but still exposed to order effects. Extra calls should therefore be spent only on windows where presentation bias is most likely to matter.

    Intuition

    If the same best/worst choice survives small reorderings, it is more likely to reflect the document set itself rather than an accidental left-to-right bias.

    P2 · Lightweight constraint

    Same-Call Worst-Signal Regularization

    Keep a head-focused TopDown bubblesort pass, but use the same-call worst output only as a local negative constraint.

    What

    The best output still drives promotion toward the ranking front. The worst output only pushes one clearly bad document to the back of the current window instead of launching a full backward ranking process.

    Why

    Standalone BottomUp is too noisy to trust globally, but the worst signal may still be informative when it is conditioned on the same query, the same candidates, and the same call as the winning best signal.

    Intuition

    Treat worst as a brake, not as a second driver. The goal is to regularize local mistakes without importing a whole noisy BottomUp channel into the final ranking.
